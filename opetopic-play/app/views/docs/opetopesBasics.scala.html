Opetopic Diagrams I

As mentioned in the introduction, opetopes can be seen as certain well-formed higher dimensional trees. It may not be surprising, therefore, that we begin by fixing some notation for trees. In particular, opetopic diagrams arise from the interaction of two methods for depicting a tree. An example of each of these methods is shown in the following diagram:

As will become clear upon inspection, both diagrams represent the same rooted tree. The picture on the right is probably the more familiar: the tree is depicted as a graph with nodes and edges. The digarm on the left, by contrast, depicts the tree structure using a collection of boxes, with the containment of the boxes specifying the descendent ordering. The correspondence between the two can be summarized by noticing that we have a bijection between the boxes on the left, and the edges on the right.

Now consider the following diagram which, unlike the previous, is not a picture of the same tree in two ways, but rather a picture of two different trees combined into a single diagram.

On the one hand, we have the tree defined by the containment of the boxes, and on the other, the tree determined by the edges. Observe that the boxes marked a, b, and c play a dual role: they are both the "leaves" of the tree formed by the boxes (since they contain no boxes themselves), while at the same time we are using them to serve as the "nodes" for the tree determined by the edges.
